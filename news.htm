About the Fiddle Man....Buddy Spicher

  photo by Jim Peters

    A native of Dubois, Pennsylvania, Buddy performed on the WWVA Jamboree in Wheeling, West Virginia.   From there he went to work with some of the great country show bands including Judy Lynn's noted western swing aggregation in Las Vegas and Tahoe prior to making Nashville his home in 1958.  Having been aptly called "the Jascha Heifetz of the country fiddle,"  Buddy's genius for arrangements and harmony  made him a favorite with Nashville record producers, while his state-of-the-art perfection with gutsy, straightforward solo work established him in the role model of America's best-known quintessential country fiddle player.  His confidence and professionalism belies a gentle nature and extreme modesty; traits which have won him many friends among a legion of fans.                 

               Photo at Wolfy's by Robert Cogswell

    One of "Music City's" most in-demand studio musicians for three decades, Buddy's versatile, precision fiddle style has made enormous contributions to the commercial success of classic earlier recordings by Webb Pierce, Ray Price; Bill Monroe;, Kitty Wells; Jimmy Martin and Hank Snow, as well as those of Bob Wills; The Lewis Family; George Strait; Linda Ronstadt; Hank Thompson; Henry Mancini; Bob Dylan; The Rolling Stones; Dan Fogelberg;  Crystal Gayle, The Osborne Brothers, and Garth Brooks, to name a few of the countless artists with whom he as worked on diverse album and single record projects.  When you hear that familiar velvet smooth fiddle in George Strait's, "Amarillo by Morning", you experience the lonesome emotion of the song as Buddy's heartfelt solo expresses it.   Buddy appeared with Mark O'Connor on his  "Heroes" album, a collection of performances by Mark and his favorite fiddle players.

    Along with his imaginative arrangements, Buddy is well known as being a master of "double stops", i.e. playing two notes at the same time and literally harmonizing with himself.  In his  performances he plays harmonized lines and solos that most people couldn't imagine playing with two fiddlers, yet he seems to do it effortlessly.   When you watch and hear Buddy play with The Nashville Swing Band, its hard to imagine how that much beautiful sound comes from a single fiddle.  

    Fiddle Camp demonstration of Ray Noble's "Cherokee"  in "double stops".  Fellow campers and  instructors look on.  Jazz violinst Sara Caswell on the left, and world renowned first violin of the Guarneri String Quartet, Arnold Steinhardt.                photos by Jim Peters

 

    There is a wonderful interview with Buddy that was conducted by Jim Christian for Fiddler Magazine, in the Fall 1998 issue.  The interview took place at a recent Mark O'Connor Fiddle Camp.    It gives much more of his background, influences and details of his career.  An excerpt.....

"My new love is jazz because there's nowhere for me to go in my own mind anyway, because country fiddle, to me, actually doesn't exist anymore.   It's bluegrass fiddle played with a rock and roll band which is the new country.  Bluegrass fiddle actually works better because it is closely associated with rock and roll because of the same type of licks.   If you listen to a rock and roll guitar, that's what they're playing.  So when I'm on a session I'll play all my bluegrass licks to match what the guitar is doing.  But  there's so many great bluegrass fiddle players, and rather  than try to compete with them, it's more a wide open field of  trying to be a poor man's Stephane Grappelli or Joe Venuti.  For one thing they play the kind of tunes that I like better, like "After You've Gone" and "Lady Be Good."  Those tunes are a lot of fun to play because you're going through a lot of chords."
